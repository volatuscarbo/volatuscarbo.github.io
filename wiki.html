Key milestones from the first international climate agreements to the EU's 2050 net-zero commitment and beyond.

International Framework

1992
Rio de Janeiro · June 1992
UNFCCC — United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change
Adopted at the UN Conference on Environment and Development ("Earth Summit"). First global framework to coordinate a response to climate change. Set the objective of stabilising GHG concentrations to prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference. Now ratified by 198 Parties. Aviation emissions not explicitly addressed.
1997
Kyoto, Japan · December 1997 (entered into force 2005)
Kyoto Protocol
First legally binding treaty with country-specific emissions reduction targets. Committed developed (Annex I) countries to reduce emissions by an average of 5% below 1990 levels over 2008–2012. Did not bind developing nations. Introduced three market mechanisms: Emissions Trading (ET), Clean Development Mechanism (CDM), and Joint Implementation (JI). Article 2.2 tasked ICAO with limiting aviation bunker fuel emissions.
2009
Copenhagen · December 2009
COP15 — Copenhagen Accord
Non-binding political agreement. Countries pledged to limit global warming to below 2°C. Introduced concept of nationally determined contributions (NDCs). Widely considered a diplomatic disappointment but established the 2°C target that Paris later codified.
2015
Paris · December 2015 (entered into force November 2016)
Paris Agreement — COP21
Adopted at COP21 (195 states participated; the EU counts as a 196th Party). Legally binding framework requiring all countries to submit NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions) and ratchet ambition every 5 years. Goals: (1) hold warming well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels; (2) pursue efforts to limit to 1.5°C; (3) strengthen climate resilience. No specific aviation provisions — ICAO tasked via CORSIA. Entered into force 4 November 2016.
2016
Montreal · October 2016
ICAO 39th Assembly — CORSIA Adopted
ICAO member states unanimously adopted Resolution A39-3, establishing CORSIA as the mechanism to deliver carbon-neutral growth from 2020 (CNG2020 — the aspiration itself was first established at the 37th Assembly in 2010 and reaffirmed at the 38th in 2013). Voluntary pilot phase to begin 2021.
2019
Brussels · December 2019
European Green Deal
Landmark European Commission strategy aiming to make the EU the first climate-neutral continent by 2050. Covered energy, transport, industry, food systems. Aviation identified as a key sector requiring decarbonisation. Led directly to the Fit for 55 legislative package.
2021
EU · June 2021
European Climate Law
Writes into binding EU law the goal of climate neutrality by 2050. Sets the interim target: at least −55% net GHG emissions by 2030 vs. 1990 levels. Establishes a European Scientific Advisory Board on Climate Change. CORSIA pilot phase begins (2021–2023). (Note: the −90% by 2040 interim target was added by amendment in 2026 — see below.)
2023
EU · May 2023
Fit for 55 Package — Final Adoption
Comprehensive legislative package revising all relevant EU policy instruments to deliver the 2030 climate target. For aviation: revised EU ETS Directive (Regulation 2023/958 and 2023/959), ReFuelEU Aviation (Regulation 2023/2405), extended MRV framework. EU ETS cap tightened; free allowances for aviation phase-out accelerated. CORSIA Phase 1 begins (2024–2026).

ReFuelEU Aviation Regulation becomes applicable from 1 January 2025 — 2% SAF blending obligation begins. Regulation (EU) 2024/2493 extends EU ETS MRV to include non-CO₂ aviation effects; aircraft operators must monitor and report non-CO₂ data from this date.

EU ETS — Overview & History EU ETS

The European Union Emissions Trading System is the world's first major carbon market, launched in 2005. It uses a cap-and-trade mechanism to reduce greenhouse gas emissions cost-effectively across the EU economy.

What Is the EU ETS?

The EU ETS sets an absolute limit (cap) on the total amount of certain GHGs that can be emitted by covered installations and aircraft operators. Within that cap, companies receive or buy emission allowances (EUAs — EU Allowances) which they can trade. At the end of each year, each covered entity must surrender allowances equal to its verified emissions. If emissions fall, surplus allowances can be sold; if emissions exceed allocations, additional allowances must be purchased.

One EUA = one tonne of CO₂ equivalent (tCO₂e).

The Emissions Cap & Linear Reduction Factor

The cap declines each year by the Linear Reduction Factor (LRF), calculated as a percentage of the average 2008–2012 baseline emissions. Following the 2023 Fit for 55 reform:

  • LRF was 1.74%/year through Phase 2 and most of Phase 3
  • Raised to 2.2%/year for 2021–2023
  • Further raised to 4.3%/year for 2024–2027
  • Then 4.4%/year from 2028 onwards
  • One-off reductions: 90 million EUAs removed in 2024; 27 million removed in 2026

Market Stability Reserve (MSR)

Established in 2019, the MSR addresses surplus allowances that had built up. The MSR absorbs allowances when the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation (TNAC) exceeds 833 million, and releases allowances when TNAC falls below 400 million. The doubled intake rate of 24% of TNAC has been maintained through 2030 as part of the Fit for 55 reform.

2023 Reform (Fit for 55)

Regulation (EU) 2023/959 amended the EU ETS Directive. Key changes for aviation included:

  • Accelerated phase-out of free allowances for aircraft operators (full auctioning by 2026)
  • 20 million allowances from the aviation cap reserved to support SAF uptake (2024–2030)
  • Extended scope to include non-CO₂ effects monitoring
  • Review of CORSIA convergence by end of 2026

EU ETS — Aviation EU ETS

Aviation was included in the EU ETS from 2012. The scheme applies to aircraft operators performing flights departing from or arriving at EEA airports. Key changes since 2023 include the accelerated phase-out of free allowances.

Scope of Aviation in the EU ETS

The EU ETS covers CO₂ emissions from all commercial flights between airports within the European Economic Area (EEA) — i.e., EU Member States plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway. Intra-EEA flights are the current scope; international flights are addressed by CORSIA.

⚠ Historical Scope Changes

Aviation was originally included for all flights to/from EEA airports (2012). This was narrowed to intra-EEA only ("stop-the-clock" derogation 2012–2016) pending ICAO's development of CORSIA. The intra-EEA scope became permanent under Fit for 55, with CORSIA covering international flights.

Interaction with CORSIA

The EU ETS and CORSIA address different flight categories:

  • EU ETS: Intra-EEA flights (CO₂ only, from 2025 also non-CO₂ monitoring)
  • CORSIA: International flights between CORSIA-participating states

The European Commission is required to publish an assessment by end of 2026 on the degree of convergence between CORSIA and EU ETS goals. If CORSIA is found to be insufficient, the Commission may propose extending EU ETS scope to international flights.

✅ Verifier Obligations — EU ETS Aviation Scope
  • Confirm the aircraft operator's administering Member State and that the correct competent authority has approved the Monitoring Plan.
  • Verify that only intra-EEA flights are included in the EU ETS Annual Emissions Report (AER); international flights should appear in the CORSIA emissions report instead.
  • Check that the operator's free allocation entitlement (if any) matches the allowances returned — noting the phase-out schedule.
  • Confirm that SAF volumes reported for allowance purposes are supported by verified SAF certificates and Proof of Sustainability documents.

⚠ Swiss Linkage — Bilateral Agreement

Switzerland operates its own ETS (Swiss ETS), linked to the EU ETS by the bilateral Switzerland–EU ETS Linking Agreement. Swiss ETS allowances (CHUs) are fungible with EU ETS allowances (EUAs). Flights between Switzerland and EEA states are treated equivalently to intra-EEA flights for EU ETS purposes. Verifiers checking operators with Swiss routes should confirm the operator is reporting under the appropriate system (Swiss ETS or EU ETS) and not double-counting.

Outermost Regions — Scope Changes Through the Years

The EU's outermost regions (ORs) — defined in Article 349 TFEU — are legally part of the EU and the EEA, but are geographically remote from the European mainland. Their airports are EU airports for EU ETS purposes, so flights between the mainland and ORs are intra-EEA in legal terms — and therefore in EU ETS scope — unless a specific exemption applies. Because air transport is often the only practical connection between ORs and the mainland, ETS treatment of these routes has been a recurring political and regulatory issue.

2012 — Full scope Included — OR airports are EU airports; all commercial flights to/from ORs were within the full-scope ETS (Directive 2008/101/EC) No special OR carve-out existed. The "stop the clock" derogation (Decision 377/2013/EU) removed only international (EEA ↔ third country) routes; OR routes were unaffected. This created an asymmetry: a Paris–New York flight was suspended from ETS (international), but a Paris–Guadeloupe flight (same airline, comparable distance) remained in ETS scope. 2017–2020 — Regulation (EU) 2017/2392 Still included — no specific OR derogation created. Operators continued to report and surrender allowances for OR routes. Free allocation for OR-serving operators remained based on the standard tonne-kilometre allocation method (no separate OR quota). Political pressure from France, Spain, and Portugal for an OR connectivity exemption was noted in Council discussions but not legislated.
2021–2023 — Art. 28a (pre-Fit for 55 reform) Still included. CORSIA pilot phase did NOT cover OR routes (CORSIA covers international routes; OR routes are intra-EEA and therefore not subject to CORSIA). Operators on OR routes had no CORSIA alternative — unlike operators on EEA ↔ third-country routes (which were derogated to CORSIA), OR operators remained fully in EU ETS with no offset alternative. The COVID-19 baseline adjustment (2019 baseline for CORSIA) did not affect EU ETS OR route reporting.
2023 Reform — Regulation (EU) 2023/958 (Fit for 55) Still included in EU ETS. The reform did not create a blanket exemption for OR routes. However, three specific changes affect OR operators:
  1. Connectivity assessment obligation: The Commission must monitor and report on the impact of the ETS free-allowance phase-out on connectivity to outermost regions (recital 19 and Art. 28c of the amended Directive). If significant connectivity harm is found, the Commission may propose targeted measures.
  2. PSO exemption retained and clarified: The existing Annex I PSO exemption (for flights operating under a PSO designation per Reg. 1008/2008) is retained. Importantly, the reform confirmed that PSO-exempt flights are excluded from both the emissions reporting obligation and the FEETS/SAF allowance mechanism — PSO exemption is a full exclusion from the aviation activity definition.
  3. Free-allowance phase-out applies equally: No separate phase-out schedule for OR routes. Operators serving ORs face the same 75%/50%/0% timetable (2024/2025/2026) as all other aviation operators. No ring-fenced free allowances for OR connectivity were legislated, despite lobbying efforts from the three Member States most affected.
2024 onwards — Current position OR routes are fully in EU ETS (intra-EEA scope). No free allowances from 2026. CORSIA does not apply to OR routes. OR-serving operators can claim SAF zero-EF and FEETS support on the same terms as all other operators. The Commission's connectivity assessment (due before the end of 2026 review cycle) may lead to future legislative proposals. Some OR routes carry significant PSO designations (particularly inter-island routes in the Canary Islands and Azores) and those specific services retain their full Annex I exemption. Operators mixing PSO and non-PSO operations on the same routes must carefully segregate PSO flights from their AER.

PSO Exemption — Scope and Limits for OR Routes

The Public Service Obligation exemption is the primary mechanism providing relief on OR routes, but it is narrower than often assumed:

AspectDetail
Legal basis ETS Directive Annex I (aviation activity definition), cross-referencing Regulation (EC) No 1008/2008 Art. 16–18 (PSO imposition procedure)
What qualifies Only flights operated under a formally designated and published PSO in the EU Official Journal under Reg. 1008/2008. The PSO must be specifically imposed on that route by the relevant Member State and notified to the Commission. Not every thin route to an OR is a PSO route.
Scope of exemption The exemption removes the flight entirely from the Annex I "aviation activity" definition — it is not merely a monitoring exemption. The flight does not appear in the AER, does not generate an allowance obligation, and does not generate FEETS entitlement.
Common OR PSO routes (examples) Inter-island routes within the Canary Islands (e.g., Las Palmas–El Hierro, Las Palmas–La Gomera); inter-island routes within the Azores (e.g., Ponta Delgada–Flores, Ponta Delgada–Corvo); some routes to/from mainland Portugal and the Azores or Madeira where no adequate sea or surface alternative exists
Non-PSO OR routes Commercial mainline and charter routes to ORs (e.g., Amsterdam–Martinique, London–Tenerife, Frankfurt–Las Palmas) are NOT covered by the PSO exemption — they are fully in EU ETS scope unless operated under a specific PSO designation, which these routes typically are not.
Operator obligation when mixing PSO and non-PSO If an operator flies both PSO-designated and non-PSO flights on the same route (e.g., one airline holds the PSO tender and also operates commercial flights), the PSO-designated operations must be clearly separated in the AER. Misclassifying a non-PSO commercial flight as PSO-exempt is a material misstatement.
✅ Verifier Checklist — Outermost Region Routes
  • Confirm OR airports in scope: All nine outermost regions are EU ETS scope (intra-EEA). Verify that the operator is not omitting OR-route flights from the AER on the mistaken assumption that they are "international" routes outside ETS scope.
  • PSO status verified for each claimed exemption: If the operator excludes any OR-route flight as PSO-exempt, obtain and review the Official Journal publication of the PSO designation for that specific route. Confirm the publication is current (PSO designations have validity periods) and that the operator holds the tender or is the designated carrier for that service.
  • No CORSIA offset for OR routes: OR routes are intra-EEA and therefore not subject to CORSIA. An operator cannot use CORSIA eligible units to offset EU ETS obligations arising from OR-route emissions — there is no CORSIA-EU ETS interaction for these flights.
  • SAF and FEETS claims on OR routes: SAF uplifted for flights to/from ORs is eligible for the MRR zero-EF claim and the FEETS support mechanism on the same basis as any other intra-EEA flight. Apply the proportionality factor at the specific OR airport if SAF is uplifted there.
  • Mixed-use operator flag: On routes where the same operator runs both a PSO-designated service and additional commercial frequency, obtain the operator's flight schedule and match it against the PSO tender to identify which specific flights are exempt. All non-PSO flights on the same route are in scope regardless of the route's general PSO status.

Small Emitter (De Minimis) Thresholds

Operators below certain thresholds are not fully excluded from EU ETS but may use a simplified monitoring approach (the "small emitters' tool") instead of the full fuel-based methodology:

⚠ De Minimis — Verifier Obligations

Even where an operator uses the small emitters' tool, the verifier must confirm: (1) the operator genuinely qualifies for the de minimis threshold — check flight logs against the 243 flights/4-month-period criterion; (2) the correct version of the small emitters' tool has been used (EUROCONTROL updates it periodically); (3) the tool's output has been correctly transferred into the AER. Do not assume that small operators are inherently low-risk — the small emitters' tool uses estimates and its results can diverge significantly from actual fuel consumption if the operator's route mix or load factors are atypical.

SAF Claim Under EU ETS — Zero Emission Factor & Proportionality Factor

When an aircraft operator uses Sustainable Aviation Fuel, the biogenic CO₂ released on combustion is assigned a zero emission factor under the EU ETS. This is consistent with the IPCC convention (biogenic carbon is accounted for at the land-use sector level, not at the point of combustion) and with RED III lifecycle accounting. The result is a direct reduction in the operator's verified emissions — and hence in the allowances it must surrender.

The Proportionality Factor (PF) for SAF — MRR Definition

In practice, SAF is uplifted at a specific airport and blended into shared fuel infrastructure. An operator cannot always trace exactly which flight burned which SAF molecules. The MRR therefore prescribes a proportionality factor to allocate the total SAF uplifted by the operator at a given airport across all flights departing from that airport during the monitoring period.

Proportionality Factor Formula (MRR Annex X)

PFi = Fueli / Σ Fuelairport, period

  • PFi — proportionality factor for flight i departing from airport A
  • Fueli — total fuel uplifted on flight i at airport A (tonnes)
  • Σ Fuelairport, period — total fuel uplifted by the operator at airport A across all flights in the monitoring period (typically the full calendar year)

SAF tonnes attributable to flight i = Total SAF uplifted at airport A × PFi

Where an operator uplifts SAF across multiple airports, the calculation is performed separately for each airport. SAF batches at different airports are not cross-allocated.

FEETS Proportionality Factor

The FEETS support pool is distributed among all eligible aircraft operators in proportion to their verified SAF use. The proportionality factor for FEETS determines each operator's share of the available allowances:

FEETS Proportionality Factor Formula

FEETS-PFoperator = SAFoperator, verified / Σ SAFall eligible operators, verified

  • SAFoperator, verified — total tonnes of FEETS-eligible SAF used by the operator on EU ETS (intra-EEA) flights in the reporting year, as confirmed in the verified AER
  • Σ SAFall eligible operators, verified — total tonnes of FEETS-eligible SAF used by all claiming operators across the EU ETS aviation sector in the same year

Operator's FEETS allocation = FEETS-PFoperator × Annual tranche of 20 M EUAs available for that year

The annual tranche of the 20 M EUAs is distributed by the Commission/EASA across the 2024–2030 period. The exact yearly allocation is set by implementing regulation. The FEETS-PF is calculated ex-post (after all AERs are verified and submitted), so operators receive their FEETS allocation after the AER submission deadline.

Because the FEETS-PF is determined after all operators have submitted their verified AERs, no operator can know in advance exactly how many allowances they will receive — only their proportional share relative to total verified SAF across the industry. This creates an incentive for early SAF adoption (larger proportional share when fewer operators claim) while remaining fair across the sector.

⚠ FEETS vs SAF Zero-EF Claim — Two Separate Benefits

An operator that uses FEETS-eligible SAF receives two distinct benefits — they must be tracked separately:

  1. Zero emission factor (MRR SAF claim): Reduces the operator's reported CO₂ in the AER, which directly reduces the number of allowances it must surrender. This is calculated using the MRR Proportionality Factor (flight-level allocation).
  2. FEETS allowance receipt: Separately, the operator receives a proportional share of the 20 M EUA pool, calculated using the FEETS Proportionality Factor (sector-wide share of verified SAF). These allowances arrive in the Union Registry account after the AER cycle and can be used for surrender or traded.

Both benefits are legitimate and intentional — the FEETS mechanism was designed precisely to make SAF economically viable by supplementing the emission-reduction benefit with an additional financial transfer. Verifiers should confirm that the SAF is correctly reflected in the AER (for the zero-EF benefit) and that the FEETS claim documentation is consistent with the verified AER.

The Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) framework defines what aircraft operators must monitor and report, and what accredited verifiers must check. This page focuses on the verifier's role.

Legal Framework

  • Directive 2003/87/EC (EU ETS Directive, as amended by Regulation 2023/959)
  • Monitoring and Reporting Regulation (MRR) — Regulation (EU) 2018/2066 (as amended)
  • Accreditation and Verification Regulation (AVR) — Regulation (EU) 2018/2067
  • Regulation (EU) 2024/2493 — extends MRV to non-CO₂ aviation effects (from 1 Jan 2025)

Monitoring Plan

Aircraft operators must have an approved Monitoring Plan (MP) before the start of any monitoring period. The MP must be approved by the competent authority. It specifies:

  • Methodology for monitoring CO₂ emissions (fuel use method — Tier 1 or Tier 2)
  • Data sources and data flow activities
  • Control activities and procedures
  • From 2025: methodology for monitoring non-CO₂ parameters
✅ Verifier Obligations — Monitoring Plan Review
  • Confirm the MP has been approved by the administering Member State's competent authority and is current (not outdated or superseded).
  • Check that the monitoring methodology applied during the year matches what is approved in the MP — any deviations must be identified and assessed for materiality.
  • Verify that control procedures described in the MP (e.g., data quality checks, IT system controls) are actually in place and operating effectively.
  • From 2025: confirm the MP includes the non-CO₂ monitoring methodology as required by Regulation (EU) 2024/2493.

Annual Emissions Report (AER)

The AER covers all EU ETS-relevant flights operated during the calendar year (1 January – 31 December). Key elements the operator must report:

  • Total CO₂ emissions (tCO₂)
  • Aggregated fuel consumption per flight type
  • Evidence of any SAF use and verified SAF quantities
  • Uncertainty assessment
  • From 2025: non-CO₂ monitoring data

Tonne-Kilometre Report

The Tonne-Kilometre (TKM) report is required when an aircraft operator applies for free allocation of allowances. It covers:

  • Revenue tonne-kilometres flown on eligible routes
  • Must be monitored in the year prior to applying for allocation
  • Maximum uncertainty: 2.5% for Tier 2 operators; 5% for Tier 1
  • TKM reports must also be verified by an accredited verifier

CORSIA ICAO Global

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) is ICAO's global market-based measure to address CO₂ emissions from international aviation. It complements the EU ETS by covering intercontinental flights.

Background & Legal Basis

CORSIA was adopted at the ICAO 39th Assembly in October 2016 (Resolution A39-3). It is the first global market-based measure for any sector. The legal framework is embedded in ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV (Environmental Protection — Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation). The scheme's stated goal is to achieve carbon-neutral growth from 2020 (CNG2020) for international aviation.

Scope

CORSIA applies to international flights between CORSIA-participating states. Purely domestic flights are excluded (those may fall under national schemes such as the EU ETS intra-EEA scope or the French domestic scheme). Aeroplane operators with annual CO₂ emissions ≤10,000 tCO₂ from international flights are exempt from offsetting obligations (but MRV may still apply above certain thresholds).

The baseline determines what level of emissions triggers offsetting obligations. All aeroplane operators on international routes must monitor CO₂ emissions from 1 January 2019 onwards under ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV. Key elements:

Operators submit an EMP to their State Authority before the monitoring period. The EMP specifies the fuel monitoring method: fuel use (from actual fuel consumption measured per flight), default fuel factors, or the ICAO CERT tool. Submitted at the end of each compliance period (3 years), the EUCR demonstrates that the operator has cancelled sufficient eligible emissions units to cover its offsetting obligations. Also requires verification.

What Qualifies as SAF?

SAF must meet the sustainability criteria defined in the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II/III), including:

  • Minimum GHG savings threshold (typically ≥65% lifecycle GHG reduction vs. fossil jet fuel)
  • No feedstock from high-biodiversity or high-carbon stock land

SAF Attribute Certificates (Book-and-Claim System)

⚠ Regulatory Limitation — Book-and-Claim

Book-and-claim SAF certificates do not confer regulatory compliance credit in any current mandatory scheme. Under EU ETS (MRR/AVR), CORSIA (ICAO Annex 16, Vol. IV), and the French compensation scheme, the aircraft operator must demonstrate physical uplift of SAF — i.e., actual delivery of certified fuel into the aircraft's tanks, evidenced by fuel delivery receipts, invoices, and a traceable chain of custody. A book-and-claim certificate alone — representing the environmental attributes of SAF uplifted elsewhere by someone else — cannot substitute for physical delivery documentation in these regulatory schemes.

Under ReFuelEU Aviation (Regulation (EU) 2023/2405), a limited form of book-and-claim is introduced for the supplier blending mandate only: fuel suppliers may use registry-tracked certificates to demonstrate compliance with their supply obligation at EU airports. However, aircraft operators reporting under Article 8 must report actual SAF uplifted; the use of attribute certificates in operator-level compliance reporting remains subject to Member State competent authority approval and cannot be assumed.

The term "book-and-claim" refers to a voluntary commercial mechanism — separate from any regulatory obligation — in which a SAF producer registers the environmental attributes of produced SAF in a registry, issues a certificate, and transfers that certificate to an airline buyer. The airline "claims" the sustainability attributes on paper without the fuel physically reaching their tanks. This is used for voluntary corporate sustainability reporting (e.g., CSRD scope 3, Science Based Targets) but does not count as SAF uplift for regulatory purposes under EU ETS, CORSIA, or the French scheme.

Reporting Obligations

Both aviation fuel suppliers and aircraft operators have reporting obligations under ReFuelEU:

  • Fuel suppliers: Annual report to the competent authority on SAF quantities supplied per airport and per airline
  • Aircraft operators: Report SAF volumes physically uplifted and integrate into EU ETS AER; note that book-and-claim certificates do not substitute for physical uplift records under EU ETS

Intersection with EU ETS

SAF use reduces an aircraft operator's net CO₂ emissions under the EU ETS. The Commission reserved 20 million EUAs from the aviation cap (2024–2030) specifically to incentivise SAF adoption. Operators using certified SAF may benefit from reduced allowance surrender obligations to the extent SAF lifecycle emissions are lower than fossil jet fuel.

✅ Verifier Obligations — ReFuelEU / SAF
  • When verifying EU ETS AERs: check that SAF volumes claimed are supported by valid sustainability certificates (ISCC CORSIA, RSB CORSIA, or equivalent scheme) and chain-of-custody documentation.
  • Confirm that the SAF emission factor used in the AER calculation corresponds to the certified lifecycle GHG value, not the fossil default.
  • Where an operator presents book-and-claim certificates: note that these do not constitute valid SAF uplift evidence under EU ETS (MRR/AVR). The verifier must obtain physical fuel delivery records to support any SAF emission factor reduction in the AER.
  • Note: ReFuelEU verification is primarily the competent authority's responsibility for fuel suppliers; verifiers' main interface is through the EU ETS AER and CORSIA emissions reports where SAF use affects calculated CO₂ emissions.

Non-CO₂ Effects From 2025

Aviation's climate impact extends well beyond CO₂. Non-CO₂ effects — including contrails, NOx, soot, and water vapour — accounted for over 66% of aviation's net climate forcing in 2018. EU mandatory monitoring began 1 January 2025.

What Are Non-CO₂ Effects?

When aircraft burn jet fuel at high altitude, they produce several non-CO₂ emissions that affect the atmosphere and climate in ways distinct from CO₂:

1. Contrails and Contrail-Cirrus

Condensation trails (contrails) form when hot, humid exhaust gases mix with cold air at cruise altitude. When atmospheric conditions are supersaturated with respect to ice, contrails persist and spread into contrail-cirrus clouds. These high-altitude ice clouds:

  • Reflect incoming solar radiation (cooling effect — mainly daytime)
  • Trap outgoing longwave (infrared) radiation (warming effect — day and night)
  • Have a net warming effect overall, because nighttime warming and daytime trapping exceed daytime cooling

Contrail radiative forcing is highly variable and uncertain — it depends on location, season, time of day, atmospheric humidity, and aircraft type.

2. NOx (Nitrogen Oxides)

NOx emissions at cruise altitude lead to two competing chemical effects:

  • Short-term: Formation of ozone (O₃) in the upper troposphere → warming effect
  • Long-term: Destruction of methane (CH₄) → cooling effect

The net effect of aviation NOx is a short-term warming dominated by ozone formation.

3. Aerosols — Soot & Sulphur

Soot (black carbon) particles from jet engines can act as seeds for contrail and cirrus cloud formation. Sulphur aerosols have a direct cooling effect. Both contribute to indirect aerosol-cloud interactions that are highly uncertain.

4. Water Vapour

At cruise altitude, water vapour from combustion can persist and contribute to cirrus cloud formation. The direct radiative forcing of aviation water vapour is relatively small compared to contrails and NOx.

EU Regulatory Framework — MRV for Non-CO₂

Regulation (EU) 2024/2493, adopted on 23 September 2024, extends the EU ETS Monitoring, Reporting and Verification framework to include non-CO₂ aviation effects. Key provisions:

  • Mandatory monitoring of non-CO₂ parameters by aircraft operators from 1 January 2025
  • Data is collected and reported but does not yet trigger allowance surrender obligations — this is a data-gathering phase to build the evidence base
  • The Commission will review the non-CO₂ data and may propose regulatory action (e.g., allowance obligations) in future based on findings
  • Operators must update their Monitoring Plans to include non-CO₂ methodology

NEATS Tool

The EU has developed the NEATS (Non-CO₂ Aviation Effects Tracking System) — jointly operated by the European Commission (DG CLIMA) and EUROCONTROL — to support operators in calculating non-CO₂ climate impacts. Version 2 (NEATS V2) allows aircraft operators to calculate the non-CO₂ climate impact of individual flights using operational data they already collect for EU ETS reporting. Key inputs: flight trajectory, fuel burn, engine type, atmospheric conditions.

Uncertainties

Non-CO₂ effects are subject to significant scientific uncertainty, particularly:

  • Contrail formation prediction (depends on local atmospheric conditions not always predictable)
  • Indirect aerosol effects on cloud formation
  • Quantification of NOx ozone vs. methane effects over different timescales
  • Effective Radiative Forcing (ERF) metrics vs. traditional GWP approaches

Current research (including EASA's non-CO₂ research programme) is working to reduce these uncertainties.

✅ Verifier Obligations — Non-CO₂ MRV (from 2025)
  • Confirm that the aircraft operator's Monitoring Plan has been updated to include a non-CO₂ monitoring methodology, as required by Regulation (EU) 2024/2493.
  • Verify that non-CO₂ parameters are being monitored and recorded in accordance with the approved methodology (e.g., NEATS V2 tool inputs or alternative approved method).
  • Check that non-CO₂ data is reported in the AER separately from CO₂ data, using the correct templates and formats required by the competent authority.
  • Note: At present, non-CO₂ data does not trigger additional allowance surrender — verification confirms data quality and completeness, not compliance with a cap.
  • Stay current with EASA and Commission guidance as the regulatory framework for non-CO₂ is actively evolving.

French Compensation Scheme France — Domestic

Article 147 of French Law 2021-1104 introduced a mandatory obligation for aircraft operators to compensate 100% of their CO₂ emissions from domestic flights as of 1 January 2024.

Legal Basis

The French compensation scheme is established by Article 147 of Law No. 2021-1104 (Loi Climat et Résilience), which amended the French Code de l'environnement. The implementing decree specifies the scope, thresholds, and reporting obligations. The scheme is administered by the DGAC (Direction Générale de l'Aviation Civile).

Scope

The scheme applies to:

  • Both commercial and non-commercial aircraft operators
  • Operators who are already subject to the EU ETS

Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) Cross-Scheme

SAF is the central decarbonisation lever for aviation. This page covers what SAF is, how it is produced, the sustainability criteria that apply, how each scheme treats it differently, certification pathways, and the verifier's role at each stage.

Knowledge Base — Fuel Taxonomy

Before diving into scheme-specific rules, it is essential to understand the fuel taxonomy that underlies every regulatory framework. The terms biofuel, advanced biofuel, SAF, LCAF, RCF, and e-fuel are often used interchangeably but have distinct regulatory meanings. Misclassification directly affects which GHG threshold applies, which certification scheme is required, and whether a verifier can accept a claim.

Level 1 — Conventional Aviation Fuel

Jet-A / Jet-A1 is a kerosene-based fossil fuel refined from crude oil. It is the baseline against which all alternative fuels are compared. Its lifecycle GHG intensity is standardised at:

  • 89 gCO₂e/MJ — ICAO/CORSIA fossil fuel comparator (Annex 16, Vol. IV, Appendix 5)
  • 94 gCO₂e/MJ — EU transport fossil fuel comparator (RED III, Annex V, Part C)

These two different baselines mean that the same physical fuel batch may have a different calculated GHG saving percentage depending on which regulatory framework is being applied — a critical detail for cross-scheme verification.

Level 2 — Biofuel (Conventional)

A biofuel is a liquid or gaseous transport fuel derived from biomass. Under EU law, the definition is established in Article 2(33) of RED III (Directive (EU) 2023/2413), which carried forward the RED II definition. Conventional biofuels are produced from food and feed crops (e.g., wheat starch, sugar beet, rapeseed oil, palm oil). In the aviation context, a conventional crop-based biofuel blended into jet fuel is technically a biofuel, but it is not eligible as SAF under ReFuelEU Aviation (Regulation (EU) 2023/2405, Articles 3(7)–3(8)), because food and feed-crop feedstocks are explicitly excluded from the definitions of SAF and aviation biofuels.

⚠ Conventional Biofuel ≠ SAF under EU Law

A biofuel produced from rapeseed, palm oil or sugar cane may meet the RED III GHG threshold but is still excluded from ReFuelEU SAF eligibility. Under Regulation (EU) 2023/2405, Article 3(7)–(8), the SAF definition and aviation biofuel definition explicitly exclude food and feed crop-based fuels. Verifiers must distinguish between RED III compliance (for renewable energy counting) and ReFuelEU SAF eligibility (for mandate compliance).

Level 3 — Advanced Biofuel

An advanced biofuel is defined in Article 2(34) of RED III as a biofuel produced from feedstocks listed in Annex IX, Part A of RED III. The EU definition of "advanced" is feedstock-based, not technology-based — this contrasts with the US approach where "advanced" refers to the production technology used.

Annex IX Part A feedstocks are considered non-competing with food/feed systems and are uncapped. They include: agricultural residues and straw, food and biowaste from urban and rural areas, municipal solid waste (non-recyclable fractions), algae, CO₂ for Power-to-Liquid processes, sawdust and forestry residues, and others.

Advanced biofuels count double towards the EU's renewable energy sub-targets (RED III, Article 22) and have a specific sub-mandate within the overall renewable transport fuel target.

Level 4 — SAF (Sustainable Aviation Fuel) under EU Law

Under ReFuelEU Aviation (Regulation (EU) 2023/2405, Article 3(7)), SAF is defined as a drop-in aviation fuel that meets one of three categories and complies with RED III sustainability criteria (Article 29 of RED III, as incorporated by reference):

What Is SAF?

Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) is a drop-in replacement for conventional fossil jet fuel (Jet-A / Jet-A1), produced from non-petroleum feedstocks such as waste oils, agricultural residues, municipal solid waste, or from renewable electricity and captured CO₂. It is chemically similar enough to fossil kerosene to be blended and used in existing aircraft and infrastructure without modification.

SAF must be certified to ASTM D7566 (the international technical specification for synthesised aviation fuel) before it can be blended with conventional jet fuel and used in commercial aircraft. Once blended, the mixture meets the regular ASTM D1655 jet fuel standard and is no longer distinguishable from fossil kerosene.

⚠ Why Is the Blend Limit 50%?

Most SAF pathways produce fuel with very low aromatic content. Conventional jet fuel requires 8–25% aromatics — the elastomer O-ring seals in aircraft fuel systems swell slightly with aromatics to prevent leakage. Too little aromatic content causes seal shrinkage and potential fuel leaks. Until 100% SAF pathways are certified with compatible aromatic content or synthetic aromatics are added, the 50% technical blend cap applies industry-wide.

Feedstock Categorisation for EU ETS Annual Emissions Reporting

When an aircraft operator reports SAF under the EU ETS Annual Emissions Report (AER), the feedstock origin and waste/residue classification are not merely administrative details — they determine whether the fuel qualifies for a zero emission factor at all, and under which certification pathway. The sections below explain how each feedstock type is classified and how that classification flows into the AER.

Animal-Derived Fats — The Three Categories

Animal fats used in SAF production are governed by two overlapping regulatory frameworks: the EU Animal By-Products Regulation (EC) No 1069/2009 (ABP Regulation), which assigns risk-based categories (Cat. 1, 2, 3) to materials of animal origin; and RED III Annex IX, which determines whether a feedstock qualifies as advanced (double-counted, uncapped or capped) or does not qualify at all. The two frameworks use different logic, and the interaction between them is critical for verifiers.

⚠️ Key verifier principle: A feedstock may be legally usable to produce HEFA fuel without qualifying for a zero emission factor under the EU ETS. The reference for zero-rating is MRR Article 38(5) → RED III Article 29(2)–(7) and (10) — not Annex IX directly. Annex IX is required for FEETS support (via ReFuelEU) and for counting toward renewable energy targets, but the zero-rating mechanism itself rests on Article 29 sustainability criteria. Category 3 animal fats could theoretically be zero-rated if they meet Article 29 in full and are certified under an approved scheme — but no current major certification scheme (ISCC EU, RSB EU RED) certifies Cat. 3 tallow for this purpose, so in practice the claim cannot be substantiated.

Why Category 3 is a Verifier Risk Area

In practice, Cat. 3 animal fats (primarily tallow rendered from slaughterhouse operations) are cheaper and more widely available than Cat. 1 & 2 fats. This creates a commercial incentive for fuel producers to blend Cat. 3 tallow into HEFA production. A supplier may market the resulting fuel as "animal fat-based biofuel" without making the category distinction explicit.

The legal basis for zero-rating under EU ETS is MRR Article 38(5) → RED III Article 29(2)–(7) and (10) — requiring the biofuel to meet land-use sustainability criteria and a minimum 65% GHG savings threshold, certified under an approved scheme. Annex IX listing is not itself the zero-rating criterion; it is the criterion for FEETS support (via ReFuelEU) and for counting toward renewable energy targets. However, no current approved certification scheme (ISCC EU, RSB EU RED) certifies Cat. 3 tallow for aviation SAF purposes — meaning that in practice, a Cat. 3-based fuel will always lack the certified evidence needed to substantiate a zero-rating claim. Verifiers must check:

  • Does the Proof of Sustainability (PoS) explicitly state that the animal fat feedstock is Category 1 or Category 2 under Regulation (EC) 1069/2009?
  • Does the certification audit trail trace the fat back to a source classified under Cat. 1 or Cat. 2 in a certified rendering facility?
  • If the PoS references "tallow" without specifying the ABP category, the verifier should request supplementary documentation from the fuel supplier confirming the category. Generic "tallow" may be Cat. 3.
  • ISCC EU and ISCC CORSIA certification documents should state the feedstock type and, for animal fats, the ABP category. If this information is absent or unclear, raise a finding.

Cat. 3 tallow has no current approved certification pathway for aviation SAF (no ISCC EU or RSB EU RED scheme covers it), so zero-rating cannot be substantiated without explicit Cat. 1/2 confirmation — the claim fails due to absence of certified evidence, not solely Annex IX status.
  • Step 4 — ReFuelEU vs. EU ETS distinction: Confirm that the PoS used for EU ETS zero-rating purposes is specifically an ISCC EU or RSB EU RED certificate (not only an ISCC CORSIA or RSB CORSIA certificate, which apply different criteria).
  • Step 5 — Verify no double-use of Cat. 3 tallow: If the operator has received SAF allowances (FEETS), confirm that the fuel volumes claimed are exclusively backed by Annex IX Part A or Part B eligible feedstocks, not Cat. 3 tallow.

Regulatory basis for finding:

  • MRR Article 38(5): Biofuels must fulfil the sustainability and GHG savings criteria of RED III Article 29(2)–(7) and (10) to be counted towards the zero-rated biomass fraction. This is the direct legal basis for zero-rating — not Annex IX listing.

Corrected total CO₂: 55,237 + 2,907 = 58,144 tCO₂
Material misstatement: 2,907 tCO₂ understatement

Annex IX Part A — Waste and Residue Feedstocks (Advanced Biofuels)

SAF Under Each Scheme — Side-by-Side

1. EU ETS EU ETS

Under the EU ETS, SAF that meets RED III sustainability criteria is assigned a zero CO₂ emission factor (biomass fraction = zero-rated). This directly reduces the number of EU Allowances (EUAs) an aircraft operator must surrender.

  • Eligibility criteria: Must meet RED III sustainability criteria — minimum 65% GHG lifecycle savings vs. the fossil fuel comparator (94 gCO₂e/MJ for transport fuels)
  • Evidence required: A Proof of Sustainability (PoS) document — typically the certification document from an approved scheme (ISCC EU, RSB EU RED, or equivalent recognised by the competent authority)
  • Monitoring Plan: SAF use must be reflected in the approved Monitoring Plan; the methodology for calculating the zero-rated fraction and applying the correct emission factor must be described
  • AER reporting: The verified AER must separately identify: (a) the total fuel quantity, (b) the SAF fraction, (c) the emission factor applied to the SAF fraction (zero), and (d) the PoS reference
  • SAF allowance incentive: 20 million EUAs reserved (2024–2030) to be allocated to aircraft operators who uplift SAF, partially covering the cost premium above fossil kerosene. First allocation: €100 million-worth of allowances distributed in September 2025.
  • Lower Carbon Aviation Fuel (LCAF): Fuels that do not reach the 65% threshold but still have lower lifecycle emissions than fossil kerosene may qualify as LCAF — these receive a partial (proportional) emission factor reduction, not full zero-rating
✅ Verifier Obligations — SAF in EU ETS AER
  • Confirm that the SAF or LCAF used has a valid PoS issued by a RED III-recognised certification scheme (ISCC EU, RSB EU RED, or competent authority-approved equivalent).
  • Verify the PoS covers the entire supply chain from feedstock origin to the point of delivery (chain of custody), with no breaks.
  • Check that the GHG lifecycle value stated in the PoS is consistent with the emission factor used in the AER calculation — do not accept a zero rating for LCAF unless the operator meets the ≥65% threshold.
  • Confirm the quantity of SAF claimed in the AER is supported by fuel delivery documents (invoices, fuel receipts, mass balance records) and matches the PoS volumes.
  • Book-and-claim certificates cannot substitute for physical SAF uplift records in EU ETS regulatory reporting. If an operator presents only book-and-claim documentation, the verifier must qualify the verification statement accordingly, as the SAF emission factor reduction is not substantiated per MRR Art. 54 and AVR requirements.
  • If the operator has received SAF allowances from the 20M EUA reserve: confirm the SAF volumes and types submitted for the allocation match the AER figures.

2. CORSIA CORSIA

Under CORSIA, SAF is referred to as CORSIA Eligible Fuel (CEF) and is split into two types:

  • SAF — meets ICAO sustainability criteria and achieves significant lifecycle GHG reduction
  • LCAF (Lower Carbon Aviation Fuel) — achieves at least 10% GHG reduction vs. the CORSIA fossil baseline but does not meet the full SAF sustainability criteria

Under CORSIA, SAF use reduces the operator's offsetting obligation. The emission reduction from SAF use is subtracted from the total CO₂ that must be offset at the end of each compliance period. In principle, sufficient SAF use could reduce the offsetting obligation to zero.

CORSIA Lifecycle GHG Calculation

The lifecycle emissions value of a CORSIA Eligible Fuel is calculated as:

LCA value = Core LCA emissions + ILUC emissions − Emission credits
Fossil baseline: 89 gCO₂e/MJ
Minimum GHG reduction required: ≥10% (i.e., LCA value ≤ 80.1 gCO₂e/MJ)
Emission reduction per MJ of SAF used = 89 − LCA value (gCO₂e/MJ)

CORSIA Sustainability Criteria (Three Pillars)

PillarKey Requirements
1. Carbon ReductionLifecycle GHG emissions ≥10% lower than fossil baseline (89 gCO₂e/MJ). Calculated using ICAO's approved LCA methodology (including ILUC for biofuels).
2. EnvironmentalNo conversion of high-carbon stock land (forests, peatlands, wetlands). Protection of biodiversity. Soil, water, and air quality criteria apply.
3. Socio-EconomicRespect for land rights, water rights, labour rights. No adverse effects on food security. FPIC (Free, Prior and Informed Consent) where applicable.

CORSIA Default Lifecycle Emissions Values

ICAO publishes and periodically updates default LCA values for common feedstock/pathway combinations (Document 06 — Default Life Cycle Emission Values, last updated June 2025). Operators may use these default values or may commission a site-specific LCA for their particular supply chain (which could yield a lower value and thus more emission reduction credit).

✅ Verifier Obligations — SAF in CORSIA
  • Confirm the SAF/LCAF used is certified under a CORSIA-recognised sustainability certification scheme (ISCC CORSIA, RSB CORSIA, or equivalent approved by ICAO Council for the relevant compliance period).
  • Verify the LCA value used: if using ICAO default values, confirm correct values from the most current ICAO Document 06. If using a site-specific LCA, confirm it was conducted by an accredited body and accepted by the State Authority.
  • Check that the emission reduction calculation formula is correctly applied: Emission Reduction (tCO₂) = (89 − LCA value) × energy content (MJ) ÷ 1,000,000.
  • Confirm CORSIA-eligible fuel volumes with physical delivery records, fuel invoices, and certificate references — ensure no volumes claimed under both CORSIA and EU ETS (double-use prevention).
  • Verify that the certification is valid for the Phase in question (Pilot Phase vs. Phase 1 vs. Phase 2) — only Phase 1-approved programmes apply to 2024–2026 compliance.

3. ReFuelEU Aviation ReFuelEU

ReFuelEU is the EU's demand-side mandate that requires aviation fuel suppliers to ensure a minimum SAF share in the fuel they supply at EU airports. Unlike EU ETS (which rewards SAF use through fewer allowances) or CORSIA (which reduces offsetting obligations), ReFuelEU sets a compliance obligation directly on the fuel supply chain.

What Qualifies as SAF Under ReFuelEU?

SAF eligible under ReFuelEU must meet RED III sustainability criteria and be produced from eligible feedstocks under RED III Annex IX (Parts A and B) or via the Power-to-Liquid (e-fuel) pathway using renewable electricity. Key exclusions — not found in RED III alone — specific to ReFuelEU:

  • Food and feed crops (no crop-based biofuels count as SAF)
  • Palm oil and all palm-derived materials (including PFAD)
  • Soy-derived materials
  • Soap stock and derivatives

GHG Threshold Under ReFuelEU / RED III

Biofuel-based SAF must achieve a minimum 65% lifecycle GHG savings compared to the fossil fuel comparator. For synthetic e-fuels, additional requirements on the source of electricity (renewable) and CO₂ (directly air-captured or from biogenic sources) apply to demonstrate climate benefit.

Book-and-Claim Under ReFuelEU

The book-and-claim mechanism allows an aircraft operator to claim SAF credits without physically uplifting SAF at the departure airport. The operator purchases a SAF certificate from a registry-approved seller (typically a fuel supplier who uplifted SAF elsewhere in the EU). Key requirements:

  • Certificates must be issued and retired through an approved registry (e.g., SAFc Registry, ISCC Credit Transfer System, RSB Book and Claim Platform)
  • Each certificate represents one tonne of SAF (or per MJ quantity) and its associated sustainability attributes
  • Certificates must not be claimed by multiple parties (unique retirement required)
  • The underlying SAF must have been physically uplifted at an EU airport within the same reporting year
  • E-fuels produced outside the EU may also be covered, subject to country-of-origin criteria
✅ Verifier Obligations — SAF in ReFuelEU Annual Report
  • Verify the aircraft operator's annual ReFuelEU report in accordance with Regulation (EU) 2023/2405 and the EU ETS AVR (Regulation (EU) 2018/2067) standards.
  • Confirm all SAF quantities reported are supported by: fuel supplier documentation (delivered by 14 February), sustainability certification references, and batch identification numbers.
  • For ReFuelEU supplier compliance only: if book-and-claim certificates are presented, verify they are issued from an EASA-approved registry (e.g., SAFc, ISCC Credit Transfer System), have been properly retired (not double-used), and correspond to actual physical SAF uplifted at an EU airport within the reporting year. Remind operators that book-and-claim certificates do not count towards aircraft operator-level SAF obligations — only physical uplift does.
  • Check that the SAF types claimed comply with ReFuelEU eligibility — in particular, confirm no palm or soy-derived SAF is being counted, even if it holds a RED III certificate.
  • Verify that the e-fuel sub-mandate quantities (from 2030) are correctly identified and that the electricity used was from renewable sources as required.
  • Ensure consistency between the ReFuelEU annual report and the EU ETS AER — SAF volumes used in EU ETS zero-rating should correspond to ReFuelEU reported SAF.
  • Submit the verification report to the operator via the EASA Sustainability Portal before 31 March.

4. French Compensation Scheme French

The French domestic compensation scheme (Article 147 of Law 2021-1104) does not have a specific SAF recognition mechanism equivalent to EU ETS zero-rating or CORSIA emission reduction credits. Operators must offset 100% of domestic flight CO₂ emissions through carbon credits. However, SAF use indirectly reduces the baseline CO₂ emissions that must be offset — if an operator uses SAF on domestic routes, their verified (or calculated) CO₂ emissions from those flights will be lower, resulting in a lower compensation obligation.

No formal SAF certification framework is defined within the French scheme itself; operators relying on SAF use to reduce their Article 147 baseline must be able to demonstrate the reduced emission figure is consistent with their EU ETS verified AER.

Two interlocking international standards form the accreditation backbone for every aviation environmental verifier. ISO/IEC 17029:2019 provides the general conformity assessment framework; ISO 14065:2020 adds GHG and environmental information-specific requirements. Together they define what it means to be a competent, impartial verification body in EU ETS, CORSIA, and ReFuelEU.

The Relationship Between the Two Standards

ISO 14065:2020 is explicitly a sector application of ISO/IEC 17029:2019. This means:

  • ISO 17029 is the foundation — it sets generic requirements for any validation/verification body in any sector (construction, food safety, financial reporting, GHG, etc.).
  • ISO 14065 is the overlay — it references ISO 17029 throughout and adds requirements specific to environmental information, particularly GHG data, that go beyond what the generic standard requires.
  • A body accredited under ISO 14065 must simultaneously comply with ISO 17029 in its entirety. The two standards are applied as a complementary pair — you cannot hold ISO 14065 accreditation without meeting ISO 17029.
  • The relevant EU legislation (AVR — Regulation (EU) 2018/2067, Article 4) mandates accreditation under both standards for EU ETS verification bodies.
  • ICAO Annex 16, Volume IV, Appendix 6, Section 2.1 mandates ISO 14065 (which incorporates ISO 17029 by reference) for CORSIA verifiers.
⚠ Legal obligation — both standards apply simultaneously

Under Regulation (EU) 2018/2067 (AVR), Article 4(1), a verifier performing EU ETS verification must be accredited by a national accreditation body in accordance with ISO 14065 (as applied by ISO 17029). Accreditation under only one of the two standards is insufficient. For CORSIA, ICAO Annex 16, Vol. IV, Appendix 6, Section 2.1 similarly requires ISO 14065-based accreditation, which inherently requires ISO 17029 compliance.

Data Gaps All Schemes

How each aviation environmental scheme defines, calculates, and resolves data gaps — and what verifiers must check when data is missing or incomplete.

⚠️ Why Data Gaps Matter

A data gap occurs when data required to calculate or report emissions (or fuel uplift) is missing, lost, or unreliable. Each scheme has different rules for detecting gaps, estimating missing values, and setting thresholds above which escalation is required. Operators are never allowed to benefit from a data gap — surrogate values must always be conservative (i.e., they must not under-estimate emissions or over-state compliance).

EU ETS — MRR Framework (Regulation 2018/2066)

The EU ETS Monitoring and Reporting Regulation (MRR) — Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2018/2066 — contains the primary legal framework for data gaps in EU ETS aviation. The relevant articles are Art. 23 (temporary tier deviation), Art. 65 (data gaps), and Art. 70 (CA conservative estimates).

Calculating the Data Gap — 6 Tracks

The European Commission's M&R Task Force has established a structured decision-tree approach with six tracks for selecting the appropriate surrogate value. Tracks are listed in order of preference. The key principle: the lower the quality of the surrogate data, the larger the required safety margin.

Track 1 — Reproducible data of the same quality

A redundant metering system or a supplier measurement of identical quality is available and operational during the data gap period.

Dr = S

No safety margin required. Dr = data to be used in reporting; S = surrogate data from redundant system.

Track 2 — Reproducible with quality loss

A secondary system or source is available, but it delivers data of lower quality than the approved tier. A safety margin is required to account for the reduced accuracy.

Activity data: Dr = S + S × (Us – Ut)
Calculation factor: Dr = S + S × (Us – Up)
If uncertainty not quantifiable: Dr = S + S × x%

Us = uncertainty of the secondary system; Ut = uncertainty of the approved tier; Up = uncertainty of the undisturbed primary system; x% = individually demonstrated safety margin.

Example (aviation): A secondary aircraft fuel meter delivers 3.7% uncertainty instead of the primary meter's 1.5% tier 4 level. Dr = S + S × (3.7% − 1.5%).

Track 3 — Lower tier / default values (calculation factors only)

Missing data is replaced by default values from the regulation or IPCC guidelines. Not applicable for activity data — only for calculation factors (e.g., emission factors, NCV).

Case 3-1a (uncertainty given): Dr = S + UL
Case 3-1b (uncertainty not stated): Dr = S + x% × S

S = default value from regulation/guidelines; UL = uncertainty as stated in that source; x% = individually demonstrated safety margin.

Track 4 — Estimation via correlating parameters

A scientifically proven correlation between the missing parameter and another measurable parameter exists. Examples: fuel input correlated with energy output; fuel density correlated with NCV.

Case 4-1 (historical simultaneous monitoring available): Dr = S + 2 × σ
Case 4-2 (proven correlation, no simultaneous records): Dr = S + x% × S

S = surrogate data from correlation function; σ = standard deviation of historical simultaneous monitoring; x% = individually demonstrated safety margin.

Track 5 — Historical records

Applied when the data gap can be estimated from long-term historical data (e.g., seasonal patterns, maintenance cycles). The operator must demonstrate that conditions did not change during the gap period.

Case 5-1a (≥20 data points): Dr = S + 2 × σ
Case 5-1b (<20 data points, calc. factors only): Dr = Smax
Case 5-2 (σ cannot be reasonably determined): Dr = S + x% × S

S = surrogate from historical statistics; Smax = maximum value of historic data set; σ = standard deviation of historic records.

Track 6 — Expert opinion

Used only when Tracks 1–5 are not applicable. An independent professional expert (not the verifier) must be engaged. The expert opinion must explicitly address conservativeness and justify why no higher emissions can be expected.

Aviation-Specific Data Gap Procedures (EU ETS)

For aircraft operators, the most common data gaps involve fuel uplift records and fuel properties. The hierarchy for filling these gaps is:

  • Missing fuel uplift data (Method A — block fuel): Use secondary sources in order of preference: (1) fuel invoice from supplier; (2) fuel receipt; (3) ATM fuel records; (4) ACARS fuel data; (5) operational flight plan (OFP) values + safety margin; (6) BADA model re-run for the full flight as surrogate.
  • Missing fuel density: Apply standard density of 0.8 kg/litre for Jet-A/Jet-A1 (this is the MRR default — no additional safety margin needed as it is already conservative for most fuel grades).
  • Missing NCV / emission factor: Use IPCC or Annex II MRR default values, adding the stated uncertainty (Track 3).
  • Missing flight data (e.g., origin/destination, aircraft type): Run the ICAO BADA model for the complete flight as surrogate activity data.

Verifier Responsibilities — EU ETS Data Gaps

✅ Verifier Checklist — EU ETS Data Gaps
  • Identify all data gaps in the annual emissions report (AER) and confirm each is disclosed in the data gap section of the Verification Report (AVR Annex I).
  • Check that the method used to fill each gap is approved by the CA in the Monitoring Plan (MP). If the method is not approved: assess whether it is conservative — if not, raise a non-compliance (Art. 65 MRR).
  • Assess whether any unapproved method leads to a material misstatement (quantitative test: exceeds materiality threshold; qualitative test: willingness to correct, likelihood of recurrence, intent).
  • For each data gap: record the size and nature of the issue, material impact, reference to the AER element and MP element concerned, and the MRR article breached (AVR Art. 27(4)).
  • Confirm the operator has a written procedure for preventing future data gaps and that control activities are in place — absence is a non-conformity.
  • Confirm that conservative surrogate values do not lead to an under-estimation of emissions — this is the fundamental test of Art. 65 compliance.

CORSIA Data Gap Procedure — Step by Step

  1. Primary monitoring (Appendix 2): Use the approved Fuel Use Monitoring Method (Method 1–5 in EMP). Record fuel use per flight.
  2. Secondary sources (Art. 2.4.1(b)): If a primary measurement fails, use secondary sources specified in the EMP (e.g., fuel invoices, fuel company records, flight computer data). These must be pre-approved in the EMP — they prevent a gap from occurring at all.
  3. CERT gap-fill: Where neither primary nor secondary sources are available, the operator using a Fuel Use Monitoring Method shall fill the gap using the ICAO CORSIA CO₂ Estimation and Reporting Tool (CERT). CERT estimates CO₂ based on Great Circle Distance (GCD) or Block Time by aircraft type.
  4. Calculate the data gap percentage: Gap % = (number of flights filled with CERT) ÷ (total international flights subject to offsetting) × 100
  5. If gap < 5%: Fill with CERT and report the percentage in the Emissions Report. Verifier notes this.
  6. If gap ≥ 5%: Operator must engage the State authority to resolve the situation before submission. State may direct the operator to use specific remediation methods.

CERT Emission Calculation Formula

When CERT is used to fill a data gap, CO₂ emissions for the missing flight(s) are calculated as:

CO₂ (tonnes) = Mf × FCFf

Where:

  • Mf = mass of fuel f used (tonnes), estimated by CERT from GCD or block time for the aircraft type
  • FCFf = fuel conversion factor: 3.16 kg CO₂/kg for Jet-A / Jet-A1; 3.10 kg CO₂/kg for AvGas / Jet-B

CERT's underlying CO₂ Estimation Models (CEMs) are a set of equations estimating fuel burn as a function of GCD or block time per aircraft type, validated against historical flight data.

ICAO-Level Gap Filling (State did not report)

If a State does not submit its annual aggregated Emissions Report to ICAO, ICAO fills the gap using its own methodology (ICAO Methodology for Gap Filling, Nov 2021):

  1. Identify operators using ICAO Doc 8585 (Designators for Aircraft Operating Agencies)
  2. Extract ADS-B data (Automatic Dependent Surveillance – Broadcast) for those operators
  3. Apply CERT to the ADS-B traffic data to estimate CO₂ per operator
  4. Aggregate emissions by State pair for inclusion in CORSIA sectoral totals and the Sectoral Growth Factor calculation
✅ Verifier Checklist — CORSIA Data Gaps
  • Confirm the Emissions Monitoring Plan (EMP) includes documented procedures to prevent data gaps and specifies approved secondary sources.
  • Calculate and verify the data gap percentage: (CERT-filled flights ÷ offsetting-eligible flights) × 100. This must be below 5%.
  • If ≥ 5%: confirm the operator has engaged the State authority and that the state has been notified.
  • For all CERT-filled flights: verify the correct CERT version was used for the reporting year; verify the aircraft type codes and route data entered are accurate.
  • Verify the Emissions Report correctly separates fuel-use-monitored emissions from CERT-estimated emissions.
  • Apply professional scepticism: a high data gap rate may indicate inadequate monitoring procedures — raise as a non-conformity if control activities are insufficient.

How to Address ReFuelEU Data Gaps

Unlike EU ETS and CORSIA, ReFuelEU does not prescribe a single method for filling missing data. The following hierarchy is the practical approach applied during verification:

  1. Fuel uplift gaps (missing uplift volume at a Union airport): Reconstruct from (1) fuel receipt / defueling slip; (2) fuel company invoice; (3) tech log fuel entries; (4) ACARS fuel message; (5) OFP fuel planned uplift ± reasonable tolerance. A safety margin should be applied when using planned values.
  2. Missing fuel category breakdown (safety fuels): If the operator cannot separately identify contingency, alternate, and discretionary fuel, the full safety fuel total must be excluded from the "required fuel" calculation — this increases, not reduces, the measured uplift gap, so it is inherently conservative from the scheme's perspective.
  3. Missing fuel density: Apply the CORSIA / MRR standard density of 0.8 kg/litre as a conservative default when converting volumetric uplift to mass.
  4. Missing SAF quantity or batch data: If SAF data is incomplete, the flight may not be counted as SAF-compliant. SAF quantity cannot be estimated — actual batch documentation (certificate, mass balance record) is required.
  5. Exemption management: Where missing data causes apparent non-compliance with the 90% uplift rule, operators must submit a justified exemption request via the EASA Sustainability Portal.

Data Collection Requirements for ReFuelEU

Operators must systematically collect and retain the following data per flight for each Union airport:

  • Fuel uplift (actual litres or kg, by fuel type)
  • Fuel density (actual or standard 0.8 kg/L)
  • Trip fuel (fuel required for the flight itself)
  • Taxi fuel
  • Block off fuel (total fuel on departure) and block on fuel (total fuel on arrival)
  • Safety fuels: final reserve fuel, contingency fuel, alternate fuel, discretionary fuel (taxi, extra, etc.)
  • SAF batch documentation: for any SAF uplifted — batch number, producer, quantity, feedstock, sustainability certification
  • Preceding flight data (where needed to justify carry-forward fuel that reduces uplift)
✅ Verifier Checklist — ReFuelEU Data Gaps
  • Request all source documents before verification: fuel receipts, OFPs, tech logs, ACARS messages, fuel invoices. Identify gaps early — do not accept "data will come later" at verification completion.
  • For each missing fuel data point, assess materiality: does it affect the 90% uplift calculation, the SAF percentage calculation, or the fuel composition report?
  • If safety fuel data is missing and cannot be reconstructed: exclude from required fuel calculation (conservative approach). Document this in the verification report.
  • Verify that SAF claims are backed by actual batch documentation — estimated or reconstructed SAF quantities are not acceptable.
  • Report all data gaps and their treatment to the operator. Unresolved material data gaps must be reflected as qualifications in the verification statement.
  • Note: From 2025, penalties apply for non-compliance with ReFuelEU requirements — data gaps that lead to non-compliance are no longer just reporting issues.

French Domestic Compensation Scheme — Article 147 Loi Climat

The French domestic compensation scheme (Article L.229-55 to L.229-60 of the French Environmental Code; Décret 2022-667 of 26 April 2022) requires aircraft operators to offset emissions from domestic flights in France. Because eligibility for the scheme is conditional on being subject to the EU ETS, and because the same MRR methodology is used to calculate emissions, data gap rules are directly inherited from the EU ETS MRR framework.

How the French Scheme Uses EU ETS Data

Operators subject to the French scheme are, by definition, also in scope of the EU ETS. Their annual emissions report must already be compiled under Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2018/2066 (MRR). The French scheme uses these same MRR-calculated emissions as the basis for the offsetting obligation:

  • Domestic emissions (flights within France) are a subset of the total EU ETS emissions report.
  • Data gaps in the EU ETS AER automatically become data gaps in the French scheme emissions declaration.
  • The same Art. 65 MRR surrogate-value hierarchy (6 tracks) applies when filling domestic flight data gaps.
  • Conservative estimates required — operators must not benefit from data gaps.

The Décret 2022-667 states explicitly that "the methodology for calculating the emissions [must be] clearly explained and referenced in order to be verifiable." This is a direct restatement of the MRR conservativeness requirement in French law.

TAROG — Undefined & Special Routes Verifier Alert

Route categories where TAROG Table B does not assign a clear, unconditional obligation — or where obligations depend on Member State discretion, ongoing negotiations, or special exemptions. These require heightened scrutiny during verification.

⚠️ Why this page exists

TAROG Table B maps city-pair categories to reporting and surrender/offsetting obligations. However, five categories of routes fall outside the standard matrix — either because they are in a legal gap (Gibraltar), subject to national discretion (EU Overseas Territories), temporarily exempted (Outermost Regions ↔ same Member State), uncertain in scope (UK ↔ EEA Outermost Regions), or subject to an additional national obligation on top of EU ETS (France domestic). When verifying an operator's Annual Emissions Report, check whether any routes below appear in the fleet and ensure the treatment is justified and consistent with the applicable authority's position.

1 — EEA ↔ Gibraltar Routes Regulatory Gap

TAROG Table B Note (C) makes explicit that flights between EEA aerodromes and Gibraltar are not currently subject to the EU ETS reduced scope. Gibraltar is no longer an EEA territory since 1 January 2021 and is not covered by the EU–UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement. Negotiations between the EU and UK regarding Gibraltar were authorised by the European Council on 5 October 2021 but had not concluded at the time of the TAROG 3rd edition (November 2023). The TAROG states it will update if any agreement changes the position.

⚠️ Current Status: No EU ETS, UK ETS, CH ETS, or CORSIA Surrender/Offsetting Obligation

Until a formal agreement is concluded and enters into force, flights between EEA airports and Gibraltar generate no surrender obligation and no offsetting obligation under any MBM covered by the TAROG. Table B shows only CORSIA in the cell with Note (C), which per Note (C) itself means the route is excluded from the reduced scope.

Verifier action: If an operator includes or excludes Gibraltar flights from their emissions report, verify that the treatment is consistent with the TAROG Note (C) position and, importantly, confirm with the administering Member State's competent authority whether the situation has changed since the last TAROG edition. Any change could affect surrender obligations retroactively from the agreement's effective date.
